Fasteignakaup af Erlendum Kaupendum

Rules for foreign buyers purchasing real estate in Iceland

Iceland applies specific restrictions on real estate purchases by non-residents (erlendum kaupendum) through the Act on the Right of Ownership and Use of Real Estate (lög um eignarrétt og afnotarétt fasteigna). Understanding these rules is essential for any foreign buyer before committing to a purchase.

EEA citizens with a declared residence in Iceland can generally purchase real estate on the same terms as Icelandic citizens. EEA citizens without Icelandic residence face restrictions — they can purchase holiday properties (sumarbústaðir) in designated areas but cannot freely purchase all types of property.

How It Works in Practice

Non-EEA citizens face the most restrictive rules: purchases generally require ministerial approval, and the types of property and uses permitted are limited. The specific rules depend on whether the buyer's home country has a reciprocal agreement with Iceland.

All foreign buyers must obtain an Icelandic kennitala (personal identification number) before completing a real estate transaction. The kennitala is required for the purchase contract, land register registration, and tax purposes. It can be obtained from Þjóðskrá Íslands by presenting a valid passport.

Capital ControlsSubstantially relaxed post-2008 crisis; verify current position before transfer

Iceland's capital controls (fjármagnshöft), imposed after the 2008 financial crisis, have been substantially relaxed but some restrictions on cross-border capital movements may still apply to certain transactions. Foreign buyers should confirm the current position with an Icelandic bank or lawyer before transferring funds.

Common Mistake: Foreign buyers sometimes proceed to offer stage without first confirming their legal eligibility to purchase the specific type of property they want. The eligibility rules differ by property type and buyer nationality — confirm eligibility first.
Expert Tip: Obtain your kennitala before beginning your property search in earnest. Without it, you cannot complete any transaction, and the application process takes a few weeks. Starting early avoids delays when you find the right property.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can non-EEA citizens buy property in Iceland?

Generally, non-EEA citizens require ministerial approval. The specific rules depend on the buyer's nationality and whether a reciprocal agreement exists between Iceland and their home country.

What is a kennitala?

It is Iceland's personal identification number, required for all real estate transactions, tax filings, and registration. Foreign buyers must obtain one from Þjóðskrá Íslands before completing a purchase.

Have Iceland's post-2008 capital controls been fully removed?

Capital controls have been substantially relaxed but some restrictions may remain. Foreign buyers should confirm the current position with an Icelandic bank or lawyer before transferring funds.
