Skipulagssvæði (Planning Zone)

Land use planning and zoning rules for Icelandic real estate

Skipulagssvæði refers to a planning zone — a designated area with specific land use rules under Iceland's Planning and Building Act (skipulags- og byggingarlög). Iceland's planning system divides land into residential, commercial, industrial, agricultural, and protected zones, each with detailed rules on what can be built and how.

Planning decisions are made at three levels: national planning strategy (landsskipulagsstefna), regional plans (svæðisskipulag), and local plans (aðalskipulag and deiliskipulag). The local detailed plan (deiliskipulag) is the most relevant document for individual property development, specifying building heights, setback distances, coverage ratios, and permitted uses.

How It Works in Practice

Iceland's volcanic and geologically active landscape means that hazard zoning (hættusvæði) is a critical planning consideration. Properties in lava flow paths, landslide-prone areas, flood zones, or areas subject to eruption risk may be in restricted zones where development is prohibited or requires special approval.

Buyers of undeveloped land or properties with development aspirations should request the relevant deiliskipulag from the local authority before purchasing. The plan will confirm what can be built, at what height, with what setbacks, and whether any restrictions apply.

Climate-related planning restrictions have grown in importance in Iceland, with updated hazard maps following recent volcanic activity (particularly on the Reykjanes Peninsula). Buyers of properties near active geological features should check the latest hazard mapping from the Icelandic Meteorological Office (Veðurstofa Íslands).

Common Mistake: Buyers of properties near active volcanic areas on the Reykjanes Peninsula sometimes underestimate the current geological risk, focusing on price and location. Check the latest Veðurstofa hazard maps before committing — restrictions and risk classifications change with ongoing volcanic activity.
Expert Tip: Request the deiliskipulag for any property with development potential before making an offer. The plan is publicly available from the local authority and defines exactly what can be built — development plans that contradict the deiliskipulag require a plan amendment, which is a lengthy process.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a deiliskipulag?

It is the detailed local planning document specifying building heights, setbacks, coverage ratios, and permitted uses for a specific area. It is the most relevant planning document for individual property development.

What is a hættusvæði?

It is a designated hazard zone — an area subject to volcanic, landslide, flood, or eruption risk — where development is restricted or prohibited.

How do I check volcanic hazard for an Icelandic property?

The Icelandic Meteorological Office (Veðurstofa Íslands) publishes updated volcanic hazard maps. These should be checked for any property on or near active volcanic systems, particularly on the Reykjanes Peninsula.
