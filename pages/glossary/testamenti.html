Testamenti

Definition: An Icelandic will that specifies how a person's estate should be distributed after death.

Overview

A testamenti in Icelandic law is a formal legal document in which a person declares how their property should be distributed upon death. It must be created with full legal capacity and clear intention, following Icelandic legal formalities.

Icelandic law recognizes both handwritten (eiginhandar testamenti) and formally executed wills. A handwritten testamenti must be dated and signed by the testator; a formal will requires witness signatures and notarization.

For validity, a testamenti must clearly identify the testator, detail the distribution of assets, and be properly signed and dated. Formal wills must be signed in the presence of witnesses who attest to the testator's capacity and understanding.

A testamenti can designate an executor to manage the estate and fulfill the testator's wishes. It can include specific bequests, charitable donations, and conditions on inheritance distribution.

Icelandic law protects certain heirs through reserved rights (erfðaréttur), meaning spouses and children cannot be entirely disinherited. Any testamenti must respect these legal protections of family members.

Key Facts

  • Formal document declaring asset distribution
  • Can be handwritten or formally witnessed
  • Must be dated and signed by testator
  • Can designate executor and conditions
  • Must respect reserved family inheritance rights

Common Mistake

Watch out: Creating an informal testamenti that fails to meet Icelandic requirements, or attempting to disinherit protected family members.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the formal requirements for a valid Icelandic testamenti?

Must be dated, signed by the testator, and either handwritten or formally witnessed and executed.

Can I change my testamenti after creation?

Yes, you can create a new testamenti or written amendment (breytingarblað) at any time.

What is erfðaréttur in a testamenti?

Erfðaréttur is the reserved inheritance right that spouses and children maintain regardless of will provisions.
