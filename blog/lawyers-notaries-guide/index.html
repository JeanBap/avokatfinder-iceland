Understanding Iceland's Legal System

Iceland uses a Nordic civil law. In practical terms, this means legal rights and obligations are primarily defined by written statutes and codes rather than by judicial precedent. Judges apply the law as written, though case law does develop interpretive guidance over time.

For individuals and businesses from common-law countries (the UK, Ireland, USA, Australia), the key differences to note are:

  • Written contracts are paramount. Courts interpret contracts strictly according to their text, with less room for implied terms than in common law.
  • Notaries have formal legal authority. Many transactions — property sales, company incorporations, estate settlements — require notarial authentication. This is mandatory, not optional.
  • Inquisitorial court procedure. In civil litigation, judges take a more active role in investigating facts than in common-law adversarial systems.
  • Codified family law. Divorce, custody and inheritance rules are set by statutory codes; there is less judicial discretion than in common-law systems.

Working with a Iceland-qualified lawyer who understands both the local system and, where relevant, EU law is essential for cross-border matters.

Lawyers vs Notaries in Iceland: What Is the Difference?

What Does a Lögmaður Do?

A licensed lögmaður in Iceland provides legal advice, drafts legal documents, represents clients in court, negotiates on behalf of clients, and handles disputes. They can advise on virtually any area of law and are bound by professional secrecy obligations to their clients. All lögmaðurs must be registered with the Icelandic Bar Association (Lögmannafélag Íslands) and hold a law degree plus professional qualification.

What Does a Notarius Publicus (known locally as notarius publicus) Do?

A notarius publicus in Iceland is a state-appointed legal official who authenticates and certifies documents. Their role is distinct from a lögmaður: while a lögmaður advocates for a client, a notarius publicus is an impartial public officer. You will need a notarius publicus for:

  • Buying or selling real property (land or buildings)
  • Incorporating a company or changing its articles
  • Certifying powers of attorney
  • Certifying wills and estate settlements
  • Legalising and apostilling documents for use abroad

Notarial fees in Iceland are typically regulated by a statutory fee scale and are calculated as a percentage of the transaction value, not an hourly rate.

What About Legal Aid?

Iceland provides state-funded legal aid for individuals who cannot afford a lawyer, subject to income thresholds and the nature of the case. Criminal defence, asylum and some civil matters (particularly family law) are commonly covered. Contact the local bar association or court for eligibility criteria.

Legal Fees in Iceland: What to Expect

Legal fees in Iceland are not uniformly regulated (except for notarial fees and, in some areas, criminal legal aid). Rates vary significantly by city, specialisation and firm size.

Typical Hourly Rates

As a general guide, expect to pay ISK 30,000–80,000/hr (€200–€540) per hour for a qualified lögmaður in Iceland. This range reflects the difference between a sole practitioner in a regional city and a partner at a top-tier firm in Reykjavik.

  • Large international firms in Reykjavik: Higher end of the range or above
  • Mid-tier domestic firms: Mid-range
  • Regional practitioners: Lower end of the range
  • Sole practitioners / local offices: Often most affordable

Fixed-Fee Services

Many straightforward matters are handled on a fixed-fee basis in Iceland, including:

  • Company incorporation
  • Simple will drafting
  • Residential lease review
  • Trademark registration
  • Debt recovery for undisputed debts

Contingency Fees

Contingency arrangements (where the lawyer takes a percentage of any damages recovered, and charges nothing if the case is lost) are permitted in some areas of law in Iceland, though the rules vary. Ask your lawyer explicitly whether this arrangement is available and what percentage they would charge.

Notarial Fees

Notarial fees in Iceland are typically calculated on a sliding scale based on the value of the transaction. For a property purchase, expect to pay between 0.5% and 1.5% of the purchase price in notarial fees, though the exact amount depends on the statutory schedule in force.

Currency and Payment

Legal fees in Iceland are payable in ISK (Icelandic Króna). Most firms accept bank transfer; some also accept card payment. Retainer payments (advance deposits held in a client account) are standard practice for ongoing matters.

Notaries (Notarius Publicus) in Iceland: A Detailed Guide

Notaries (known locally as notarius publicus) play a central role in Iceland's legal system. Unlike in common-law countries, where notarisation is a relatively minor authentication service, Iceland's notarius publicuss are senior legal professionals with state authority over specific classes of legal transaction.

When Do You Need a Notary in Iceland?

You will need a notarius publicus for the following types of transaction in Iceland:

  • Real estate: Sale and purchase deeds, mortgages, and easements must be notarially authenticated to be legally valid. Without notarial authentication, a property transfer cannot be registered.
  • Company law: Incorporation of most company types, amendments to articles of association, and shareholder resolutions often require notarial form.
  • Family and succession: Marriage contracts, adoption proceedings, wills and probate are commonly notarised in Iceland.
  • Powers of attorney: Powers of attorney for property transactions or legal proceedings require notarial authentication to be recognised by courts and registries.

How to Find a Notary in Iceland

Notaries (notarius publicuss) in Iceland are typically listed in a separate register maintained by the notarial chamber or the Ministry of Justice. AvokatFinder includes notary offices as a separate category — search with the "notary" filter to find notarius publicuss in your city.

How Much Does a Notary Charge in Iceland?

Notarial fees in Iceland are generally regulated by a statutory fee schedule, unlike lawyer fees which are freely negotiated. Fees are usually calculated as a percentage of the transaction value. For guidance, request a fee estimate from the notary before the appointment; they are required to provide this on request.

How to Verify a Lawyer's Credentials in Iceland

Protecting yourself from unqualified practitioners is straightforward if you follow these steps:

  1. Request bar registration number. Any qualified lögmaður in Iceland should be able to provide their registration number immediately.
  2. Cross-check with the bar register. Visit the Icelandic Bar Association (Lögmannafélag Íslands) and search for the lawyer's name and registration number.
  3. Check for disciplinary history. Most bar registers note whether a member has faced disciplinary action. A suspension or strike-off is a serious red flag.
  4. Confirm professional indemnity insurance. Licensed lawyers in Iceland are required to maintain professional indemnity insurance. Ask for confirmation before engaging.
  5. Avoid cash-only arrangements. All fees should be payable to the law firm's client account, not personally to the lawyer in cash.

How the Bar Association Regulates Lawyers in Iceland

Every licensed lögmaður in Iceland is subject to oversight by the Icelandic Bar Association (Lögmannafélag Íslands). Understanding how regulation works helps you know what protections you have as a client.

Admission to the Bar

To practise as a lögmaður in Iceland, a lawyer must hold a recognised law degree, complete a period of supervised practice (typically one to three years as a trainee), pass a professional qualification examination, and be admitted to the bar. Foreign lawyers who trained outside Iceland must pass an aptitude test or fulfil re-qualification requirements before they can practise independently.

Continuing Professional Development

Registered lögmaðurs in Iceland are required to complete a minimum number of continuing professional development (CPD) hours each year to maintain their bar registration. This ensures that practising lawyers keep up to date with legislative changes, new case law and evolving professional standards.

Professional Indemnity Insurance

All licensed lögmaðurs in Iceland must maintain professional indemnity insurance to cover claims arising from negligent advice or errors in legal work. This insurance protects clients who suffer financial loss due to a lawyer's mistake. Always ask your lawyer to confirm their insurance status before instructing them.

Complaints and Disciplinary Procedures

If you have a complaint about a lögmaður in Iceland, you should first attempt to resolve it directly with the lawyer or their firm. If that fails, you can submit a formal complaint to the Icelandic Bar Association (Lögmannafélag Íslands). Serious misconduct can result in suspension or permanent disbarment. The bar association maintains a public record of disciplinary sanctions.

Client Funds and Accounts

Lawyers who handle client money in Iceland must maintain separate client accounts, segregated from the firm's own funds. This is a regulatory requirement. If a lawyer asks you to transfer money to a personal account rather than a firm client account, treat this as a serious warning sign.

Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) in Iceland

Not every legal dispute needs to go to court. Iceland has a developed framework for alternative dispute resolution (ADR) that can save significant time and cost.

Mediation

Mediation involves a neutral third party helping disputing parties reach a voluntary settlement. It is widely available in Iceland for commercial disputes, family law matters (particularly divorce and custody) and neighbour disputes. Mediated agreements are enforceable once formalised in a written settlement agreement, which a lögmaður can draft and review.

Arbitration

Commercial arbitration is an established dispute-resolution mechanism in Iceland, particularly for cross-border contracts. Arbitration clauses are commonly included in commercial agreements, and awards are enforceable internationally under the 1958 New York Convention. Iceland has arbitration institutions that can administer both domestic and international proceedings.

Online Dispute Resolution

For consumer disputes under €5,000 within the EU, the European Commission's Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) platform is available. Consumers in Iceland can use this platform to resolve cross-border e-commerce disputes without needing a lögmaður, although legal advice is recommended for complex matters.

Should You Go to Court?

Courts in Iceland can be slow and expensive. Before committing to litigation, ask your lögmaður whether mediation or arbitration is a realistic alternative. In many cases, a well-structured ADR process achieves a faster and cheaper resolution than going to trial — and preserves the business relationship where that matters.

Immigration lawyers in Reykjavik and other major cities can advise on visa categories, eligibility criteria, documentation requirements and appeal rights if an application is refused.

Property Purchase

Buying property in Iceland as a foreign national involves several legal steps, all of which require both a lögmaður (to review and negotiate the contract) and a notarius publicus (to authenticate the final transfer deed). Foreign buyers should also understand the tax implications of property ownership in Iceland, including any applicable transfer taxes, annual property taxes and rules on rental income.

Starting a Business

Foreign nationals wishing to establish a company in Iceland need legal advice on the most appropriate corporate structure, the registration process, regulatory requirements and, where applicable, sector-specific licencing. The notarius publicus plays a role in company formation: most company types in Iceland require notarial authentication of the incorporation documents.

Employment and Labour Rights

Expatriate employees in Iceland should be aware of their rights under local employment law, which applies regardless of the nationality of the employer or employee. Key areas include minimum notice periods, redundancy entitlements, non-compete clauses, and the enforceability of employment contracts governed by foreign law.

Inheritance and Estate Planning

EU Regulation 650/2012 (the European Succession Regulation) allows EU citizens to elect that their estate be governed by the law of their country of nationality rather than the country where they reside. This has significant implications for expatriates in Iceland. Cross-border estate planning requires specialised legal advice from a lawyer with expertise in both Iceland's succession law and the law of your home country.

Ten Practical Tips for Hiring a Lawyer in Iceland

  1. Check bar registration first. Verify credentials via the Icelandic Bar Association (Lögmannafélag Íslands) before paying any fees.
  2. Get a written fee agreement. Never start work without a signed engagement letter specifying fees, scope, and disbursements.
  3. Clarify who will handle your file. At large firms, a partner may pitch and a junior associate may do the work. Know who you are actually hiring.
  4. Ask about timelines. Get a realistic estimate of how long your matter will take — and insist on regular updates.
  5. Understand the billing cycle. Monthly invoices with time-recording breakdowns are standard. Review each invoice promptly and query any entries you do not recognise.
  6. Keep copies of everything. Store all correspondence, contracts, and legal opinions securely. Your lawyer's file belongs to them; your copies belong to you.
  7. Ask about alternatives to court. Mediation and arbitration are often faster and cheaper. Ask your lögmaður whether ADR is appropriate for your matter.
  8. Language matters. If you do not speak Iceland's language, engage an English-speaking lögmaður from the outset. Translation of documents is an additional cost; budget for it.
  9. Second opinions are fine. For significant matters, getting a second legal opinion from a different firm is entirely normal and often valuable.
  10. Understand conflict checks. A reputable firm will check for conflicts of interest before taking your case. If they do not, ask them to confirm they have done so.

Glossary of Legal Terms You Will Encounter in Iceland

When working with a lögmaður in Iceland, you may encounter the following legal terms. Understanding them will help you communicate more effectively and follow the progress of your matter.

Power of Attorney
A legal document authorising one person (the attorney-in-fact) to act on behalf of another (the principal) in legal or financial matters. In Iceland, powers of attorney for property transactions must be authenticated by a notarius publicus.
Affidavit
A written statement confirmed under oath or by solemn affirmation. Affidavits are used in court proceedings and administrative processes as evidence of facts.
Due Diligence
The investigation of a business, property or individual before entering a contract or completing a transaction. Legal due diligence reviews contracts, litigation history, regulatory compliance and title to assets.
Injunction
A court order requiring a party to do — or to stop doing — a specific act. Interim injunctions can be obtained urgently to prevent ongoing harm while the main case is resolved.
Statute of Limitations
The maximum period after an event within which legal proceedings may be brought. In Iceland, limitation periods vary by type of claim. Missing the limitation period generally bars your claim entirely.
Notarisation / Apostille
Notarisation is the authentication of a document by a notarius publicus. An apostille is a certification added to a notarised document to make it recognised in countries party to the Hague Convention — useful for documents that need to be used internationally.
Lien
A legal claim over a property as security for a debt or obligation. In Iceland, mortgage liens are registered in the land registry and must be discharged before a property can be sold with clean title.
Retainer
An advance payment made to a law firm, held in a client account and drawn down as work is completed and invoiced. Retainers are common practice for ongoing legal relationships in Iceland.
